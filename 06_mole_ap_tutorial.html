Introductory General Chemistry   Unit 06

The Mole & Molar Relationships

In this unit you will learn how chemists count atoms using the mole, convert between mass, moles, and particles, calculate percent composition, and determine empirical and molecular formulas from experimental data. These skills are the quantitative foundation for everything that follows in chemistry.

6.1 What Is a Mole?

A mole is simply a counting unit — just like the word "dozen" means exactly 12 of something, the word "mole" means exactly 6.022 × 10²³ of something. We use the mole because atoms are far too small to count individually, so chemists count them in this enormous group instead. Avogadro's number was specifically chosen so that one mole of any element has a mass in grams that is numerically equal to its atomic mass in amu. This makes the periodic table directly usable for mass calculations.

6.022 × 1023
particles per mole — this is called Avogadro's Number (symbol: NA)
Scale check
If you counted one particle per second without stopping, it would take you 19 quadrillion years to count to the value of 1 mole — that is about 1.4 million times longer than the age of the universe.
Grains of sand
All of the grains of sand on every beach on Earth total approximately 7.5 × 10¹⁸ grains. That number is still about 80,000 times smaller than the value of 1 mole — showing just how enormous Avogadro's number truly is.
Why this specific number?
Avogadro's number is defined so that exactly 12 grams of carbon-12 (12C) contains exactly 1 mole of carbon atoms. This anchors the entire system of atomic masses to real, measurable masses in the lab.
Moles ↔ Particles
n = N / Nₐ    ⟺    N = n × 6.022 × 10²³
6.2 Molar Mass

The molar mass (symbol: M) of a substance is the mass of exactly one mole of that substance, expressed in units of g/mol. For a single element, the molar mass is simply its atomic mass from the periodic table (in g/mol instead of amu). For a compound, you calculate the molar mass by multiplying each element's atomic mass by the number of times that element appears in the formula, then adding all the results together.

Moles ↔ Mass
n (mol) = m (g) / M (g/mol)    ⟺    m = n × M

6.3 The Mole Map

Every mole conversion you will ever do flows through the central unit of moles. You cannot go directly from a mass in grams to a number of particles — you must convert to moles first, and then to particles. Think of moles as the mandatory "hub" that connects all three forms of measurement. Master these three two-way pathways and you can solve any mole problem:

For gases only, there is one additional conversion pathway: at Standard Temperature and Pressure (STP, defined as 0°C and 1 atm), exactly 1 mole of any ideal gas occupies 22.414 liters. This molar volume connects moles to volume, but it only applies to gases — not liquids or solids — and only at STP conditions. Like the other conversions, it still passes through the moles hub.

6.4 Percent Composition

Percent composition by mass tells you what fraction of a compound's total mass comes from each element. It is expressed as a percentage. You can calculate it from a known formula using the molar mass, or you can determine it experimentally from laboratory data (such as combustion analysis). Either way, the formula is the same:

% Composition by Mass — How to Calculate It
% element = (number of atoms of that element × atomic mass of that element) / molar mass of compound × 100

Worked example — Water (H₂O, M = 18.015 g/mol):

%H = (2 atoms × 1.008 g/mol) ÷ 18.015 g/mol × 100 = 11.19% hydrogen

%O = (1 atom × 15.999 g/mol) ÷ 18.015 g/mol × 100 = 88.81% oxygen

Notice that the percentages must add up to exactly 100% — this is a built-in check on your arithmetic.

◉

Exam Tip: Percent composition is the critical bridge between experimental laboratory data (masses collected during a combustion experiment) and the empirical formula (the simplest whole-number ratio of atoms in the compound). You will almost certainly encounter this connection on exams, so be sure you can work in both directions — from formula to percent, and from percent back to formula.

6.5 Empirical & Molecular Formulas

The empirical formula of a compound gives the simplest possible whole-number ratio of the atoms of each element present. For example, the empirical formula CH₂O means that for every 1 carbon atom there are 2 hydrogen atoms and 1 oxygen atom — but it does not tell you how many total atoms are in one actual molecule.

The molecular formula gives the actual number of each type of atom in one molecule of the compound. The molecular formula is always a whole-number multiple of the empirical formula. That multiplier is called n.

The Key Relationship Between Empirical and Molecular Formulas
Molecular Formula = n × (Empirical Formula)    where n = Mmolecular / Mempirical

To find an empirical formula from percent composition data, follow these five steps every time:

Step 1 — Convert percent values to grams by assuming a 100 g sample

When you assume you have exactly 100 g of the compound, each element's percent value becomes a mass in grams directly. For example, if a compound is 40.0% carbon, then in a 100 g sample you have 40.0 g of carbon. This trick eliminates the percent sign and gives you real masses to work with.

Step 2 — Convert each element's mass in grams to moles

Divide each element's mass by its molar mass (atomic mass from the periodic table, in g/mol). This converts grams into moles, which puts all elements on the same "counting" basis so you can compare them fairly.

Step 3 — Divide all mole values by the smallest mole value

Find whichever element has the fewest moles, then divide every element's mole count by that number. This rescales the ratios so the smallest value becomes 1.000. The resulting numbers are your preliminary atom ratios.

Step 4 — Round or multiply to get clean whole numbers

Your ratios from Step 3 should be very close to whole numbers (within ±0.05). If a ratio is close to a whole number, round it. If a ratio ends in approximately .5 (like 1.5 or 2.5), multiply every ratio by 2. If it ends in approximately .33 or .67, multiply by 3. These are the subscripts in your empirical formula.

Step 5 — Find the multiplier n to get the molecular formula (if given the molar mass)

Calculate the molar mass of your empirical formula. Then divide the given molecular molar mass by the empirical formula molar mass: n = Mmolecular / Mempirical. Round n to the nearest whole number, then multiply every subscript in your empirical formula by n to get the final molecular formula.

Example 1 · Mole–Mass–Particle Chain

Problem: How many molecules of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) are contained in a 25.0 g sample? And what is the total mass of only the oxygen atoms in that same sample?

This problem requires a two-part chain: mass → moles → molecules, and then a separate percent composition calculation for the oxygen question.

Step 1 — Calculate the molar mass of glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆)

Add up each element's contribution: 6 carbons × 12.011 g/mol = 72.066; 12 hydrogens × 1.008 g/mol = 12.096; 6 oxygens × 15.999 g/mol = 95.994. Total molar mass = 180.156 g/mol. You need this number before you can do any conversion.

Step 2 — Convert the mass of the sample to moles of glucose

Use the relationship n = m ÷ M. Divide the sample mass by the molar mass: n = 25.0 g ÷ 180.156 g/mol = 0.1388 mol of glucose. Notice that the grams unit cancels, leaving you with moles.

Step 3 — Convert moles of glucose to individual molecules

Multiply by Avogadro's number: N = 0.1388 mol × 6.022×10²³ molecules/mol = 8.36 × 10²² molecules. The unit "mol" cancels, leaving you with molecules.

Step 4 — Find the mass of oxygen in the sample using percent composition

First calculate %O in glucose: %O = (6 × 15.999) ÷ 180.156 × 100 = 53.29%. This means 53.29% of any glucose sample's mass is oxygen. Then multiply: mass of O = 0.5329 × 25.0 g = 13.32 g of oxygen in the sample.

Example 2 · Empirical Formula from Combustion

Problem: A 1.000 g organic compound containing only carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen is burned completely. The combustion produces 1.466 g of CO₂ and 0.600 g of H₂O. The compound's molar mass is approximately 88 g/mol. Determine the molecular formula of the compound.

In combustion analysis, all of the carbon in the original compound ends up in CO₂, and all of the hydrogen ends up in H₂O. Any oxygen in the original compound is found by subtraction after accounting for C and H.

Step 1 — Find the moles of carbon from the mass of CO₂ collected

Each molecule of CO₂ contains exactly 1 carbon atom, so moles of CO₂ = moles of C. Convert: 1.466 g CO₂ ÷ 44.010 g/mol = 0.03331 mol CO₂ = 0.03331 mol C. The mass of carbon in the sample was 0.03331 mol × 12.011 g/mol = 0.4000 g C.

Step 2 — Find the moles of hydrogen from the mass of H₂O collected

Each molecule of H₂O contains 2 hydrogen atoms, so moles of H = 2 × moles of H₂O. First: 0.600 g H₂O ÷ 18.015 g/mol = 0.03330 mol H₂O. Then: 0.03330 × 2 = 0.06661 mol H. The mass of hydrogen in the sample was 0.06661 × 1.008 = 0.06714 g H.

Step 3 — Find the mass and moles of oxygen by subtraction

Because the compound contains only C, H, and O, any mass that is not C or H must be O. Mass of O = 1.000 g total − 0.4000 g C − 0.06714 g H = 0.5329 g O. Then convert to moles: 0.5329 g ÷ 15.999 g/mol = 0.03331 mol O.

Step 4 — Find the simplest whole-number ratio (empirical formula)

Divide every mole value by the smallest mole value (0.03331): C = 0.03331 ÷ 0.03331 = 1.000; H = 0.06661 ÷ 0.03331 = 1.999 ≈ 2; O = 0.03331 ÷ 0.03331 = 1.000. The ratio is C:H:O = 1:2:1, giving an empirical formula of CH₂O with an empirical molar mass of 30.026 g/mol.

Step 5 — Use the given molar mass to find the multiplier n and the molecular formula

Divide the given molecular molar mass by the empirical molar mass: n = 88 g/mol ÷ 30.026 g/mol = 2.93 ≈ 3. Multiply every subscript in CH₂O by 3: molecular formula = C₃H₆O₃. (This compound is pyruvic acid, found in cellular respiration.)

Example 3 · Multi-Step Conversion at STP

Problem: A sample of CO₂ gas contains 3.50 × 10²⁴ molecules. What volume does this sample occupy at STP?

You are starting with a number of particles and need to reach a volume. You must pass through moles — you cannot go directly from particles to liters. This is a two-step conversion: particles → moles → liters.

Step 1 — Convert the number of molecules to moles

Divide the number of molecules by Avogadro's number: n = 3.50×10²⁴ molecules ÷ 6.022×10²³ molecules/mol = 5.81 mol of CO₂. The unit "molecules" cancels, leaving you with moles.

Step 2 — Convert moles of gas to liters at STP

At STP, every mole of any ideal gas occupies exactly 22.414 L. Multiply: V = 5.81 mol × 22.414 L/mol = 130.2 L. The unit "mol" cancels, leaving you with liters. Notice this is a very large volume — about the size of a small bathtub — because 5.81 moles is a large amount of gas molecules.
